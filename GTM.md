# GTM

The exact target user is an Engineering Manager, Head of Engineering, or CTO at a seed to Series A startup with a 5-50 person team. This person may not hold the company card personally, but they approve, recommend, or strongly influence which AI tools the team buys and renews. The pain is specific: when they open invoices for Cursor, Copilot, Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or API usage, they can tell the total is growing, but they have no benchmark for whether the plan mix is rational or whether they are quietly paying for overlap, underused seats, or the wrong tier.

Right before wanting SpendLens, they are likely to search for very practical questions rather than broad finance advice. Five realistic search queries are:

- "Cursor vs Copilot pricing for teams"
- "ChatGPT Team worth it for startups"
- "Claude Team minimum seats pricing"
- "how to reduce OpenAI API cost startup"
- "AI tool spend benchmark for engineering teams"

They hang out in places where engineering leaders and startup operators compare tools and budgets in public: `r/ExperiencedDevs`, `r/startups`, `r/SaaS`, Hacker News, Indie Hackers, CTO Craft, Rands Leadership Slack, and SaaS Alliance. On X they are likely to follow Gergely Orosz, Lenny Rachitsky, Jason Lemkin, Elad Gil, and operators who write about burn and procurement. The newsletters most likely to reach them are Lenny's Newsletter, The Pragmatic Engineer, SaaStr, and a16z growth/operator content.

The first 100 users in 30 days can come from focused distribution rather than broad launch noise. Week 1: post a teardown on X and LinkedIn showing a real or realistic audit with "$40k/year of AI overspend hidden in plain sight," tag Lenny Rachitsky and Gergely Orosz, and submit a version to `r/ExperiencedDevs` with the angle "I built a free tool that found $500/mo in AI waste at my last company." Week 2: DM 50 engineering managers or CTOs from recent Product Hunt launches and YC company directories with a short note: "We built a free audit that flags overpay on Cursor, Copilot, ChatGPT, and Claude in under 2 minutes. Want me to run your stack through it?" Week 3: post anonymized audit screenshots in Indie Hackers and Hacker News with the message "Not another AI app - this one just tells you where you are wasting money." Week 4: ask every user who finds more than $300/month in savings to share their result link internally or publicly, then repost the strongest examples with specific numbers.

The unfair distribution channel is Credex itself. A random indie hacker can build the calculator, but Credex already understands which companies are overspending, already talks to buyers who care about AI credits, and already benefits when high-savings users want a consultation. Credex can seed SpendLens into existing customer conversations, use vendor knowledge to keep pricing assumptions sharp, and turn the tool into a warm-intent funnel rather than a cold-content project.

If this is working in week 1, I would expect about 75 completed audits, 12-18 emails captured, 3-5 consultation bookings, and at least 5 organic shares or reposts of audit results. Those are not huge numbers, but they would be strong signals that the value proposition resonates with the exact kind of buyer Credex wants.
